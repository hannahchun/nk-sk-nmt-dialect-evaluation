# Automatic Evaluation Metrics

## CHRF : character n-gram F-score for automatic MT evaluation

[chrF](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3049/) evaluates n-gram overlap between reference and candidate sentences at the _character level_. It's language- and tokenization-independent. Most importantly, it provides fine-grained sensitivity to _morphological_ and _inflectional_ variability in contrast to BLEU, which weights exact token or word n‑gram matches.

According to [Popović (2015)](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3049/), chrF3 ($\beta = 3$) has been shown to achieve higher correlation with human judgments than the standard chrF ($\beta = 1$) and commonly used metrics such as [BLEU](https://aclanthology.org/P02-1040.pdf), [TER](https://aclanthology.org/2006.amta-papers.25/), and [METEOR](https://aclanthology.org/W05-0909.pdf).

$$
\mathrm{chrF}_{\beta} = (1+\beta^2) \cdot \frac{\mathrm{CHRP} \cdot \mathrm{CHRR}}{\beta^2 \cdot \mathrm{CHRP} + \mathrm{CHRR}}
$$

where CHRP and CHRR are the average character n-gram precision and recall across all n-gram orders (1–6 by default).

### Character n-gram Precision (CHRP)

For each character n-gram order (e.g., 1-grams, 2-grams, …, 6-grams), precision is computed as

$$
P_n = \frac{\text{number of matching character n-grams}}{\text{number of character n-grams in the hypothesis}}
$$

Measures how many character n-grams generated by the model (hypothesis) also appear in the reference at that particular n-gram order.

The final character precision is obtained by averaging across all n-gram orders:

$$
\mathrm{CHRP} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} P_n
$$

### Character n-gram Recall (CHRR)

Similarly, recall is computed separately for each character n-gram order:

$$
R_n = \frac{\text{number of matching character n-grams}}{\text{number of character n-grams in the reference}}
$$

Measures how many character n-grams in the reference are successfully captured by the hypothesis at that particular n-gram order.

The final character recall is obtained by averaging across all n-gram orders:

$$
\mathrm{CHRR} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} R_n
$$

### The $\beta$ parameter

A parameter that controls the trade-off between precision and recall. Recall is weighted $\beta$ times more than precision.

### Corpus-level vs. Sentence-level chrF

chrF can be computed at either the sentence level or the corpus level.

- **Sentence-level chrF**

  Sentence-level chrF computes a separate score for each hypothesis–reference sentence pair.

  Reference₁ ↔ Hypothesis₁ → chrF₁ \
  Reference₂ ↔ Hypothesis₂ → chrF₂ \
  Reference₃ ↔ Hypothesis₃ → chrF₃ ...

  Sentence-level scores are useful for analyzing individual translation examples. However, it can be unstable, especially for short sentences where a small number of character differences may substantially affect the score.

- **Corpus-level chrF**

  Corpus-level chrF evaluates an entire collection of hypothesis–reference pairs simultaneously.

  Instead of computing a chrF score for each sentence and averaging those scores, corpus-level chrF first aggregates character n-gram statistics across all hypothesis–reference pairs.

  Then, it computes corpus-level precision and corpus-level recall to output one final corpus chrF.

  Because character n-gram counts are aggregated across the entire corpus before precision and recall are computed, corpus-level chrF is generally more stable and less sensitive to variation in individual sentences, particularly very short ones.
